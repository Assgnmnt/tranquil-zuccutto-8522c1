The 5 Stages Every Woman on Assignment Goes Through

By Jackie Parker April 2025 10 min read

One of the most powerful things you can do for yourself right now is this: figure out where you actually are.

Not where you wish you were. Not where you feel like you should be. Where you actually are — on the journey from the moment God showed you something to the moment you're fully walking in it.

Because here's what I've seen over and over again in the women I work with: they're treating a Stage Two problem with a Stage Four solution. Or they're stuck in Stage Three and calling it Stage One. They're working on the wrong thing entirely — not because they lack effort, but because they haven't accurately located themselves.

Your assignment journey isn't random. It has a pattern — five distinct stages that every woman moves through. And knowing which one you're in isn't just interesting information. It's the thing that tells you what your next move actually is.

"Most women aren't stuck. They're just working on the wrong stage. Knowing where you are changes what you do next."

The Five Stages of the Assignment Journey

Stage 01

Awakening

"I sense that something is shifting — but I can't fully name it yet."

This is the beginning. Something has changed — or is changing — inside you. You feel restless despite success. You feel misaligned despite achievement. God is speaking, but the assignment hasn't yet taken shape into something you can define or describe.

This stage can feel confusing, even unsettling. Women in Awakening often describe feeling like they're in a fog — knowing something is there, but not being able to see it clearly yet.

What you need at this stage: Clarity tools and a framework to help you move from feeling to naming. You don't need a plan yet — you need language for what God is showing you.

Stage 02

Discernment

"I can feel it and describe it — but I'm not sure I'm ready to commit."

At this stage, the assignment has a shape. You can talk about it, at least in broad terms. The direction is clearer. The calling feels more real. But something is still keeping you from full commitment — and it's not confusion anymore.

Women in Discernment often look like they're doing their due diligence. They're researching, praying, planning, waiting for confirmation. Some of that is wise. But some of it is delay dressed in the language of wisdom.

What you need at this stage: A decision. Not more information — a decision. The gap between Discernment and the next stage is almost always crossed by committing, not by gathering more clarity.

Stage 03

Agreement Barrier

"I know what I'm supposed to do. But something keeps stopping me from doing it."

This is the most common stage I see — and also the most misunderstood. Women in Stage Three have clarity. They know their assignment, or at least they know the direction. The problem is not information. The problem is an internal agreement that is functioning as a governor on their movement.

They've made a deal with fear — and fear has been dressed up so well that it looks like discernment, responsibility, wisdom, or faith. The delay feels justified. That's exactly what makes it so effective.

What you need at this stage: To identify and name the specific agreement operating in your life. Once you name it, you can break it. Once you break it, you can move. This is the core work of The Discovery Lab.

Stage 04

Detachment

"I know I need to let go of what I've built — and it's the hardest thing I've ever done."

This stage is where the assignment requires something concrete from you: releasing what you've built in order to build what you were made for. It might be a title, a salary, a position, a role — or the identity that came with all of those things.

For high-achieving women especially, this is one of the most difficult stages. The ladder has been a significant part of who you are. Stepping off it — even toward something better — requires a kind of grief and courage that most people around you won't understand.

What you need at this stage: Structure. A clear plan for what you're building as you release what you're leaving. And a community of women who have made or are making the same transition — so you don't have to do it in isolation.

Stage 05

Activation

"I'm moving — but I need consistency, accountability, and systems to sustain it."

Activation is not the end of the journey — it's the beginning of a new one. Women in this stage are executing. They've made the decision, broken the agreement, done the letting go. They're in motion.

But motion is not the same as momentum. The risk at Stage Five is inconsistency, isolation, and assignment drift — slowly moving away from the original mandate without realizing it. Many women stall here not because they stopped caring, but because they stopped having structure.

What you need at this stage: Systems, accountability, and a community of women who are also building. The assignment doesn't sustain itself. You need the infrastructure to sustain you.

A Few Important Things to Know About These Stages

You don't move through them in a straight line. Most women revisit stages — particularly Stage Three. You can be in Activation in one area of your assignment and still battling an Agreement Barrier in another. That's normal, not failure.

The longest delays tend to happen at Stage Three. Not because it's the hardest stage, but because it's the most disguised. Women in Stage Three often think they're in Stage One. They're searching for clarity they already have, because naming the agreement feels more threatening than staying confused.

Knowing your stage tells you what to do next. A woman in Awakening needs different support than a woman in Detachment. A woman in Discernment needs a different conversation than a woman in Activation. When you know where you are, you stop applying generic solutions and start taking targeted action.

The most powerful thing you can do right now is locate yourself accurately. Not where you hope you are. Not where you feel you should be. Where you actually are — so you can take the next right step from there.
