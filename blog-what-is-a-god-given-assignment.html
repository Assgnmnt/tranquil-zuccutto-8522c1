What Is a God-Given Assignment — and How Do You Know You Have One?

By Jackie Parker April 2025 8 min read

Most women describe it the same way.

There's a knowing. Quiet at first — almost easy to dismiss. But it doesn't go away. It shows up in the margins of your busiest days. It surfaces in conversations that make you feel most alive. It follows you into rooms where everything looks successful from the outside, but something inside keeps whispering: this isn't it.

If you've felt that — you're not imagining it. And you're not alone.

What you're feeling is the beginning of what I call a God-given assignment. And understanding what it actually is — and isn't — is the first step toward moving toward it.

Let's Start With What an Assignment Is Not

An assignment is not a general feeling of restlessness. It's not the vague sense that you want to "do more" or "make a difference." Those are real feelings, and they matter — but they aren't an assignment. They're the preconditions for one.

An assignment is also not your job title. Not your current role, your career track, or your industry. You may work in finance and your assignment has nothing to do with finance. You may be a pastor and your assignment extends beyond the walls of your church. Your assignment lives underneath the work — not inside it.

And — this one matters — an assignment is not a dream. Dreams are things you hope for. An assignment is something you were sent to do. The difference isn't semantic. It changes everything about how you pursue it.

"Dreams are things you hope for. An assignment is something you were sent to do. The difference changes everything about how you pursue it."

So What Is a God-Given Assignment?

A God-given assignment is a specific, purposeful mandate placed inside you — not by your own ambition, but by the One who made you. It's directional. It's tied to a specific group of people, a specific problem, or a specific gap in the world that only you can fill in the way you were designed to fill it.

It has three consistent characteristics.

1. It Keeps Coming Back

You can suppress an assignment. Delay it, redirect it, rationalize your way around it. But you cannot kill it. A God-given assignment is persistent by design. It resurfaces every time you get quiet enough to hear it. That persistence isn't pressure — it's confirmation.

2. It's Bigger Than You're Comfortable With

Here's something I've noticed in my own journey and in the women I work with: a God-given assignment almost always exceeds what you believe you're currently qualified for. That's not an accident. If you could do it on your own strength, you wouldn't need the One who gave it to you.

The discomfort you feel when you think about your assignment? That's not a sign you're wrong. That's often a sign you're exactly right.

3. It's Connected to Your Story and Your Gifts

The things that broke your heart weren't random. The skills you've developed over a lifetime weren't accidental. A God-given assignment is almost always found at the intersection of what you've experienced, what you know, and what you can't stop caring about.

For me, that intersection looked like this: 30 years of human capital strategy at the highest corporate levels, a deep understanding of why talented people stay stuck — and a personal reckoning with my own delayed obedience. The Assignment Room™ didn't come from nowhere. It came from the collision of all of those things.

Your assignment is found at the intersection of what you've been through, what you know how to do, and what you cannot stop caring about. When those three things align — pay attention.

How Do You Know You Have One?

I get asked this often, and I want to give you a real answer — not a vague one.

You know you have a God-given assignment when you can say yes to at least three of these:

You feel restless despite outward success. Everything looks right from the outside, but something inside keeps pulling. You've achieved what you set out to achieve — and it's not enough. Not because you're ungrateful, but because you sense there's more.

You keep returning to the same idea. It might show up as a business, a ministry, a book, a movement, a program — but the idea is persistent. You've tried to talk yourself out of it. You've buried it under busyness. It comes back anyway.

You feel energized when you talk about it. Even when it's inconvenient. Even when it scares you. There's a particular aliveness that shows up when your assignment is the topic — and you know the difference between that and ordinary excitement.

Others have named it before you did. The people who know you best — who see you when you're most yourself — have probably already said something. "You should do something with that." "You light up when you talk about this." What they're reflecting back to you is worth noting.

You're more afraid of not doing it than doing it. The fear of being at the end of your life having never moved toward it becomes louder than the fear of failure. When that shift happens, your assignment is ready for you even if you don't feel ready for it.

What Happens When You Know But Haven't Moved

This is where most women are when they find me. They know. They've known for a while. But they haven't moved — not fully, not consistently — and they've been living in the gap between knowing and going.

That gap has a name. And it's not confusion.

It's negotiation.

You've been negotiating with your assignment. Using language that sounds like wisdom — timing, finances, preparation, responsibility — to justify a delay that is rooted in something else entirely.

I know because I did it too. For years I was operating at the highest levels of a Fortune 500 company, leading people across 40 countries, and carrying my assignment around like a secret I was too afraid to say out loud.

When my position was eliminated in January 2025, I stopped negotiating.

"The gap between knowing and going isn't confusion. It's negotiation. And negotiations can be ended."
