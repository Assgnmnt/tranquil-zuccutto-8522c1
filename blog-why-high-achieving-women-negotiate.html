Why High-Achieving Women Negotiate With Their Purpose

By Jackie Parker April 2025 9 min read

Let me describe someone to you.

She's accomplished. Genuinely accomplished — not just on paper. She leads well, thinks strategically, delivers results that other people talk about. She's built a career that most people would look at and say: she made it.

And she's stuck.

Not stuck in the way that looks stuck from the outside. She's still moving, still producing, still showing up. But there's a part of her that hasn't moved in years — maybe decades. The part that knows what God showed her. The part that has been quietly, persistently, patiently waiting for her to say yes.

If that's you — I want to tell you something important.

You are not confused. You are not unready. You are negotiating. And once you name that, everything changes.

Why High-Achieving Women Specifically

Here's the thing about high achievers: the same skills that make you exceptional at your work also make you exceptionally good at negotiating with yourself.

You are analytical. You weigh risks. You build cases. You're used to making decisions based on evidence and strategy — and when it comes to your assignment, you have turned those same tools against yourself.

You've built a compelling argument for why now isn't the right time. And it's a good argument. Logical. Reasonable. Even responsible-sounding. The problem is that it's not true — it's just convincing.

"The same skills that make you exceptional at your work also make you exceptionally good at negotiating with yourself."

There's something else that makes high achievers particularly vulnerable to this pattern: the ladder.

You've climbed it. Maybe you're near the top. And stepping off it — even toward something better — feels like a loss you haven't been given permission to grieve. Your identity is tied to the rungs. Your security lives in the structure. And your assignment is asking you to build something entirely different.

That's not weakness. That's a very human response to a very real tension. But it's a tension that has to be named before it can be resolved.

The Four Agreements That Keep You Stuck

In my work with women on assignment, I've identified four internal agreements that appear again and again — four contracts that feel like wisdom but function like walls.

The Time Agreement

"I'm too late. The window has passed. If I was going to do this, I would have done it by now."

The truth: Assignments don't have expiration dates. They have seasons. And the season you're in right now — with everything you've accumulated, everything you've learned — may be exactly the one you needed to arrive here.

The Security Agreement

"I can't afford the risk. I have responsibilities. People are depending on me. I'll move when things are more stable."

The truth: Things will never be more stable. Stability is not a season — it's a story you tell yourself while you wait. The provision for your assignment rarely appears before the obedience. It tends to show up during it.

The Qualification Agreement

"I'm not qualified for this. Someone else could do it better. Who am I to think I'm the one?"

The truth: You were not given an assignment that matched your current qualifications. You were given one that requires you to become who you were made to be. The gap between where you are and what the assignment requires? That's the curriculum, not the disqualifier.

The Comparison Agreement

"Someone is already doing this. The market is saturated. My version won't be different enough to matter."

The truth: No one can do your assignment the way you were designed to do it. Not because you're the most talented, but because you are the only one with your exact combination of experience, perspective, and calling. The comparison is a distraction, not a diagnosis.

What Makes These Agreements So Dangerous

Here's why these four agreements are particularly insidious: they don't feel like fear. They feel like discernment.

They sound like the voice of a wise mentor. They use the language of responsibility and maturity. They wrap themselves in the clothes of faith — "I'm just waiting on God's timing" — while actually functioning as a refusal to move.

This is the negotiation. Not a dramatic rejection of your calling — just a quiet, ongoing conversation with yourself about why today isn't the day. Why not yet. Why soon, but not now.

I sat in that conversation for years. I was operating at the highest levels of a Fortune 500 company, leading thousands of people across 40 countries, and I was having that conversation with myself every single day. I dressed it up in language about timing and stewardship and responsibility.

It was fear. Sophisticated, well-dressed, well-reasoned fear. But fear nonetheless.

How You End the Negotiation

You end it the same way you end any negotiation: by naming the terms and deciding they no longer serve you.

The first step is simply identifying which agreement is yours. Not all four — usually one or two are driving the delay. When you can say, out loud, "I have made an agreement with the Security Agreement, and it has been operating as my reason not to move" — something shifts.

Because named things lose power. What you can see, you can address. What stays unnamed stays in control.

"Named things lose power. What you can see, you can address. What stays unnamed stays in control."

The second step is replacing the agreement with a decision. Not a feeling, not a plan, not a vision board — a decision. A specific, stated commitment that supersedes the agreement you've been operating under.

This is the work we do inside The Assignment Room™. Not inspiration. Not motivation. Identification and decision. That's what produces movement.

A Word to the Woman Who Is Ready

If you read this and recognized yourself — if you saw your agreement named in one of those four descriptions — I want you to know something.

That recognition is not condemnation. It's an invitation.

You are closer than you think. The negotiation can end today — not because circumstances change, but because you decide they no longer get to be the deciding factor.

Your assignment is still there. It has been there this whole time. It waited through the career, the titles, the ladder-climbing. It's patient. But you don't have to make it wait any longer.
